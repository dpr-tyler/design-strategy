<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

The AI era has made the feature factory problem dramatically worse, not better. When generation models can produce working code in seconds, the bottleneck is no longer engineering capacity — it's knowing what to build. Cagan's insistence on discovery before delivery has become the central strategic discipline: with the cost of building approaching zero, the cost of building the wrong thing is now the only cost that matters. Teams that skip discovery and sprint straight to shipping are not moving fast — they are generating expensive waste at machine speed.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Four Big Risks

Before any team commits engineering effort, Cagan argues they must honestly confront four risks that kill products. Value risk asks whether customers will actually buy or use the solution — it is the most commonly skipped test, especially on internally-championed ideas. Usability risk asks whether real users can figure out how to use it without a support call or a walkthrough from the founder. Feasibility risk asks whether the engineering team can build what discovery has envisioned, given time, skill, and technical constraints that exist right now. Business viability risk is the most politically charged: even if the product works and customers love it, will legal, finance, sales, or leadership allow it to ship and scale? Running all four before writing production code is the discipline that separates product companies from feature factories.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Most product failures are not engineering failures — they are discovery failures. The four risks exist to force that reckoning before a single sprint begins.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Product Trio

Cagan's empowered team is not a PM who writes tickets and throws them over the wall. Discovery is done collaboratively by three roles working in parallel: the product manager who owns value and viability, the product designer who owns usability and the customer experience, and the tech lead who owns feasibility and flags what is technically naive before it gets mocked up. The trio meets daily in discovery — not in standups about status, but in working sessions where assumptions are tested and ideas are killed cheaply. When the trio is fractured — when designers are brought in after requirements are written or engineers are consulted only at sprint planning — the work degrades into sequential handoffs, each stage adding cost and losing fidelity. The trio's shared ownership of the discovery process is what makes "full-stack" innovation possible without chaos.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Discovery is not a phase — it is a continuous, collaborative practice. The trio does not hand off; they think together.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Empowered Product Teams vs. Feature Factories

The defining question Cagan poses is this: does your team own a problem or a roadmap? Feature factories are given a prioritized list of things to build and measured on whether they shipped them. Empowered teams are given a business outcome to achieve — reduce churn, increase activation, expand into a new segment — and trusted to discover and deliver the best solution. The distinction sounds simple but it is organizationally radical: it transfers authority from stakeholders and executives to the people closest to the customer and the technology. In a feature factory, the PM is a project manager with a better title. In an empowered team, the PM is responsible for outcomes that cannot be faked by shipping features nobody uses.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Give teams a problem to solve, not a solution to build. The roadmap is an output of discovery — not an input to it.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Discovery Techniques

Discovery is not research theater — it is a rapid, cheap, iterative process of invalidating bad ideas before they become expensive commitments. Cagan's toolkit includes customer interviews (to understand the problem, not validate the solution), prototype tests (the smallest possible artifact that can answer the question at hand), concierge tests (doing the service manually before automating it), and spikes (small technical experiments to de-risk feasibility). The guiding principle is that most ideas should fail in discovery, not in production — a failed prototype costs a day; a failed product costs a quarter. Teams that run discovery well develop an institutional tolerance for fast failure because they see it as the mechanism that makes their eventual commitments far more reliable.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** If your ideas always succeed in production, you are not testing enough of them in discovery. Failure in discovery is the system working correctly.
</FrameworkCallout>

## OKRs for Product Teams

Cagan's treatment of OKRs is specifically about resisting the corruption of outcome metrics into output metrics. "Increase trial-to-paid conversion by 15%" is an outcome OKR — it specifies what changes in the world if the team succeeds. "Ship the new onboarding flow" is a disguised project plan wearing OKR clothing. The practical test is whether the key result could be achieved by multiple different solutions — if there is only one way to hit it, it is a task, not an outcome. OKRs set this way force discovery: the team must figure out which interventions actually move the metric, which generates learning even when individual bets fail. Used properly, OKRs are the mechanism by which leadership sets direction without micromanaging method.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** An OKR that can only be achieved by one specific feature is not an OKR — it is a mandate in disguise. Outcomes leave room for the team to discover the solution.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**Outcome over output.** Shipping is not succeeding. A team that ships twelve features a quarter and moves no business metric has accomplished nothing of value — it has produced artifacts. The discipline of tying every commitment to a measurable outcome is what distinguishes product work from project work, and it requires organizational courage to hold when stakeholders are demanding visible progress.

**The team closest to the customer has the best ideas.** Cagan is explicit that the problem with most roadmaps is not poor prioritization — it is that the ideas come from the wrong people. Executives and stakeholders are too far from the friction to generate reliable solutions. The best product ideas emerge from teams who talk to customers weekly, feel the support queue, and live inside the data. The PM's job is to create the conditions for that proximity, not to serve as a relay station between management and engineering.

**Discovery reduces the cost of being wrong.** Every organization has a budget for being wrong — it is measured in time, money, and morale. Discovery is not a luxury for well-resourced teams; it is the mechanism that stretches that budget. Running ten cheap experiments to find one strong bet is not slower than committing to one idea and building it — it is dramatically faster, because the one strong bet ships with far higher confidence and requires far less iteration after launch.

**The best PMs are missionaries, not mercenaries.** Mercenary PMs optimize for shipping, for stakeholder approval, for visible output. Missionary PMs are obsessed with the problem — they would solve it regardless of the specific solution, the technology, or the quarterly priority list. That obsession is what gives them the credibility to push back on bad ideas and the judgment to know which discoveries are worth pursuing past early resistance.
