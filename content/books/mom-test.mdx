<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

In an era when AI can generate a prototype in an afternoon, the bottleneck has shifted decisively from building to knowing. Teams now ship faster than they learn, which means the cost of validating the wrong assumption has never been higher. Fitzpatrick's framework is a direct counter to this — a discipline of slowing down the conversation so you stop collecting comfort and start collecting evidence.

---

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Mom Test

The book's central premise is deceptively simple: structure your customer conversations so that even your mother — who loves you and wants you to succeed — cannot give you a false positive. The trap most founders fall into is asking questions that invite encouragement: "Do you think this is a good idea?" or "Would you pay for something like this?" These questions put the respondent in an awkward position where honesty feels unkind. The Mom Test sidesteps this entirely by keeping questions anchored to past behavior and observable facts. Ask about their life: how they currently solve the problem, how much time or money they spend on it, what they tried before. You are not testing your idea — you are testing whether the problem is real, frequent, and worth solving.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Ask questions your mom can't lie about — anchor every question to past behavior, not future hypotheticals.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Good vs. Bad Questions

The distinction between a good and a bad customer question is whether it allows the respondent to give you useless data comfortably. Bad questions are future-facing and opinion-seeking: "Would you use this?", "Do you think that's a fair price?", "How much would you pay?" These feel like research but produce noise. Good questions are retrospective and specific: "Tell me about the last time you ran into this problem," "What did you do about it?", "How much did that cost you — in time and money?" The shift forces the conversation onto facts the respondent actually knows, rather than predictions they're improvising to be polite. It also reveals the real competition: whatever they're doing right now to get by.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** "Tell me about the last time..." is almost always a better opening than "Would you ever...". Past behavior is data; future intention is flattery.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Three Bad Data Types

Fitzpatrick identifies three types of signals that feel productive but carry no information: compliments, fluff, and hypotheticals. Compliments ("That's a great idea!") are social lubrication, not market validation. Fluff is generic enthusiasm that doesn't attach to any specific problem or behavior — "I'd definitely use something like that" from someone who has never once tried to solve the problem. Hypotheticals are conditional futures: "If it did X, I would consider Y." The dangerous thing about all three is that they are not lies — the person means them in the moment — but they are systematically unreliable predictors of real behavior. Training yourself to notice when you're collecting these types is half the work of good customer discovery.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Compliments, fluff, and hypotheticals are the three bad data types — they feel validating but carry no predictive signal. Flag them and redirect.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Commitment and Advancement

The clearest signal that a customer conversation has produced real learning is whether it ends in commitment or advancement. Commitment means the person gives you something that has cost — their time (a follow-up meeting), their money (a pre-order or deposit), or their reputation (an introduction to someone whose opinion they care about). Advancement means the conversation moves your process forward in a concrete way. A conversation that ends in enthusiasm but no next step is a dead end, regardless of how positive it felt. This framework protects you from the pipeline of warm leads that never convert — the meetings that felt great but produced nothing. When you ask for commitment and it doesn't come, that silence is its own data point.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Real signal comes when someone gives time, money, or reputation. Enthusiasm without commitment is noise.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

## Past behavior predicts; stated intentions flatter

What people have already done is the most reliable predictor of what they will do. Stated intentions — "I would definitely pay for that," "We'd switch from our current vendor immediately" — are not predictions, they are performances of goodwill. The question "Have you ever tried to solve this?" and its follow-up "What did you actually do?" will reveal more about real demand than any hypothetical pricing question. If no one has tried to solve the problem at all, that is meaningful data.

## Every conversation should either learn or advance

A customer conversation that produces neither new information nor a concrete next step is a waste of two people's time. Before any call, Fitzpatrick recommends setting a goal: what specific beliefs do you need to confirm or refute, and what commitment would make this conversation a success? This discipline prevents the common trap of scheduling meetings to feel busy — to generate the sensation of progress without the substance of it. If you can't answer what a successful conversation would look like, you're not ready to have it.

## The mom test should feel like a conversation, not an interview

Rigid question scripts signal that you're extracting data, which triggers guardedness in the respondent. The best customer conversations feel like genuine curiosity — you're interested in how they work, not surveying whether your idea is good. This means listening more than talking, following unexpected threads, and resisting the urge to pitch. When something surprising comes up, that is often the most valuable signal. The interview format closes down; the conversation format opens up.

## Avoid the three failures of bad research

Fitzpatrick names three ways founders corrupt their own research: talking to the wrong people, asking bad questions, and then misreading the answers. You can do rigorous work on all three and still miss the market, but most failures trace back to the first — founders interview people who are easy to access rather than people who represent the target. A warm response from someone who isn't the customer is worthless data dressed up as validation.
