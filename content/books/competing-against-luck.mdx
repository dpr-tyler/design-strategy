<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

AI product teams are drowning in behavioral data and starving for causal explanation. Every recommendation engine, personalization system, and usage dashboard produces correlation — patterns in what people do — without answering the more important question of why they do it. Christensen's Jobs to Be Done theory is the corrective: it insists that the unit of analysis is not the user, the segment, or the behavior, but the situation — the specific circumstance in which a person is trying to make progress and reaches for a solution. In a world where AI can identify patterns in data at unprecedented scale, the teams that win will be the ones who understand which patterns actually explain causation, because only causal understanding produces products that people hire reliably instead of abandon.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Job to Be Done

The job to be done is the fundamental unit of Christensen's theory: the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. It is not a task, not a goal, and explicitly not a demographic — a 45-year-old suburban parent and a 22-year-old urban commuter may have the same job in the same moment, while two 45-year-old suburban parents may have completely different ones. The power of the framework is that it shifts the question from "who is our customer?" to "what situation produces the demand for our product?" This reframe has enormous practical consequences: it changes what you study (situations, not segments), what you build (progress, not features), and how you compete (own the job, not the category). Jobs are stable across time even as the products that serve them change — people hired carriages, then cars, then Uber for the same underlying job of getting somewhere they couldn't walk.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** The job is defined by the situation and the desired progress, not by the product or the person. Demographics describe who might have the job — they do not explain why the job exists or what a good solution looks like.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Milkshake Study

Christensen's most famous case study illustrates the gap between traditional market research and jobs-based thinking with disarming clarity. McDonald's wanted to sell more milkshakes. Traditional segmentation — surveying milkshake buyers about their preferences — produced incremental tweaks that moved no needle. The jobs-based investigation revealed something entirely different: morning buyers were overwhelmingly commuters hiring the milkshake for a long, boring drive. The job was "keep me occupied and not hungry until lunch, with one hand free for the steering wheel." The milkshake was competing with bananas, bagels, and candy bars — not with other milkshakes. Afternoon buyers were a different population entirely: parents buying a treat for a child, who then regretted how long it took the child to finish. Same product, two entirely different jobs, requiring two entirely different design solutions. No segmentation scheme based on who bought the milkshake could have surfaced this — only understanding the situation could.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Your product's real competitive set is not defined by category — it is defined by every other solution a customer might hire to do the same job. Competitors you have never considered may be taking share you cannot see.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Functional, Emotional, and Social Jobs

Every job has at least three dimensions, and the most important ones are rarely the functional ones. The functional job is the practical task: get me from A to B, track my expenses, organize my calendar. The emotional job is how the person wants to feel — or not feel — in the process of making that progress: confident, in control, not embarrassed. The social job is about how the person wants to be perceived by others while doing the job: competent, sophisticated, responsible. Christensen argues that great products address all three, and that social and emotional jobs are frequently more powerful purchase drivers than functional ones. This has direct implications for product design: a product that solves the functional job perfectly but generates anxiety (complexity, opacity, the fear of doing it wrong) will be abandoned for a less capable solution that makes the customer feel competent.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** When a product that is functionally superior loses to a functionally inferior one, the explanation is almost always emotional or social. Check the dimensions you are not measuring.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Job Spec

Christensen's job specification is the structured articulation of a job that makes it actionable for product teams: "When I [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [desired outcome]." The three parts are not interchangeable. The situation grounds the job in a real-world context that can be observed and tested. The motivation captures the progress the person is seeking — not the feature they want, but the move they are trying to make. The desired outcome names what success looks like from the customer's perspective, which is the measure against which every design decision should be evaluated. Writing a tight job spec is harder than it looks: teams consistently slip from situation into persona ("when I am a busy professional"), from motivation into feature request ("I want to click one button"), and from desired outcome into vanity metric ("so I can use the app more"). A good job spec should make it immediately obvious which solutions are worth exploring and which are not.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** A job spec written in customer language, grounded in a real situation, and oriented toward a genuine desired outcome is more valuable than any persona document — because it tells you what to build, not just who to build it for.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Hiring and Firing

Customers do not simply adopt new products — they fire existing solutions first. Understanding what a customer fires when they hire your product is one of the most revealing and underused diagnostics in product strategy. The fired solution reveals the specific failure mode of the status quo: what was intolerable, what friction crossed a threshold, what moment of frustration finally produced the switch. That is the exact terrain your product must win on to earn and hold the hire. Equally important is the ongoing risk of being fired: if a customer's situation changes, if the job evolves, or if a new solution addresses the job better on any of its three dimensions, the customer will fire your product without ceremony or explanation. Retention is not about loyalty programs — it is about continuing to make the best available progress on the job, in every iteration.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Study what customers fire as carefully as what they hire. The fired solution is a precise map of the minimum bar your product must clear — and the ongoing standard you must maintain.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**Correlation vs. causation in customer data.** Demographics and behavioral patterns correlate with purchases but do not explain them. Knowing that your most active users are 28-35-year-old urban professionals tells you where to find more customers — it tells you nothing about why they hired your product or what would cause them to fire it. Jobs-based analysis is a causal theory: it explains the mechanism. Teams that make product decisions from correlation alone are pattern-matching against noise; teams that understand the job are working from a model of causation they can test and extend.

**The job doesn't change — the products that do the job do.** This is the long-run implication of Christensen's framework. The job of "communicate with someone far away quickly" has existed for centuries and will continue to exist indefinitely. What changes is which product does the job best in a given era — telegraph, telephone, email, SMS, voice note. Understanding this distinction reframes competitive strategy: you are not competing against specific products, you are competing for the job. If you own the job deeply enough — if you understand its functional, emotional, and social dimensions better than anyone — you can survive multiple technology cycles.

**Understanding the job is a competitive moat.** Proprietary technology can be replicated; brand can be eroded; distribution can be disrupted. But a deep, institutionalized understanding of a job — built through years of customer interviews, hire/fire analysis, and iterative product development — is genuinely hard to copy. It lives in the organization's habits of inquiry, not in its codebase. Companies that have built this understanding consistently outperform their technically superior competitors because they are solving for something their competitors cannot clearly see.

**Non-consumption is the largest market.** Christensen repeatedly points out that the most common competitive alternative is not a named competitor — it is doing nothing, tolerating the problem, or developing an imperfect workaround. Teams that benchmark against existing solutions miss the far larger opportunity of converting non-consumers who have the job but have never found a solution worth hiring. These customers are not comparing your product to a competitor; they are comparing it to their current tolerance for the problem. That is a very different conversation, and it often produces far simpler, lower-cost products than the ones companies build while watching each other.

<SectionLabel>The one thing</SectionLabel>

Define the job, and you define the product — because every decision about what to build, what to cut, and what to improve becomes answerable by a single question: does this help the customer make the progress they hired us for?
