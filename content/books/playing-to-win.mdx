<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

Most product strategy conversations are actually roadmap conversations dressed up as strategy. Lafley and Martin's framework insists on something more rigorous: strategy is a set of choices that are mutually reinforcing, constrain each other, and together constitute a theory of winning. In the AI era, where the pace of product development has accelerated dramatically, the temptation is to move fast in all directions. Playing to Win argues that the opposite is required: precise choices about where to compete (and where not to) become more valuable, not less, when the speed of execution is high. Fast execution of unfocused strategy is expensive.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Strategy Choice Cascade

Martin and Lafley organize strategy into five interconnected choices that cascade from the most abstract to the most operational. **Winning aspiration** defines what winning looks like for the company — not a financial target, but a statement of where the company aims to be relative to customers and competitors. **Where to play** specifies the arenas where the company will compete: geographies, customer segments, product categories, distribution channels. **How to win** defines the source of competitive advantage in those arenas — cost leadership, differentiation, or a specific capability. **Core capabilities** are the activities and competencies the company must have to win in the chosen arenas. **Management systems** are the structures, processes, and measures that enable and reinforce the capabilities. Each choice constrains the others — good strategy is internally consistent.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Strategy is not a choice — it is a cascade of choices that must be mutually reinforcing. Any choice that is inconsistent with the others undermines the whole system.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Where to Play

The "where to play" choice is often underappreciated. It specifies where the company will and will not compete — which is as important as what it chooses to do. Defining the playing field requires choices across: customer segments (who specifically is the target customer?), geography (which markets?), channels (how will products reach customers?), categories (which products and services?), and value chain stages (which activities will the company own?). The most common failure is not having a view on this at all — competing wherever customers appear, which is a strategy for average performance.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Where not to play is as strategically important as where to play. Saying no to attractive opportunities outside your chosen arena is how you maintain focus and reinforce your advantage.
</FrameworkCallout>

## How to Win

The "how to win" choice defines the specific source of advantage that the company will cultivate in its chosen arenas. Martin and Lafley are explicit that advantage must be real, specific, and defensible — not generic. Generic advantages (we win by having great people, by innovating, by being customer-centric) are not advantages because everyone claims them. Real advantages are specific: a cost structure that competitors cannot replicate, a brand that commands premium pricing in specific segments, a distribution relationship that competitors cannot access, a feedback loop that improves with scale. The test is whether the advantage is actually causal — whether it actually drives customer choice in the target arena.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Advantage must be specific enough to be falsifiable. "We win by being better" is not a theory of advantage. "We win because our supply chain delivers 48 hours faster in this segment" is a theory.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Reverse Engineering Test

Martin's most practically useful tool is testing strategy by reverse-engineering from it. For each strategic choice, ask: what would have to be true for this choice to be correct? List the conditions — about customers, competitors, capabilities, and context — that would need to hold. Then evaluate how confident you are in each condition. This makes strategic assumptions explicit and testable. It reveals which conditions are load-bearing for the strategy and which aspects of the strategy are robust across many different scenarios.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Identify the conditions under which your strategy works. Test those conditions explicitly. Strategy that has never been stress-tested against its own assumptions is a gamble dressed up as a plan.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**Strategy as integrated choices.** The test of good strategy is coherence: each choice should reinforce the others. A company that chooses to win through cost leadership needs capabilities in operational efficiency, management systems built around cost control, and a "where to play" that is large enough to justify scale. A company that chooses differentiation needs capabilities in innovation and brand, management systems that tolerate uncertainty, and a "where to play" that includes customers willing to pay for differentiation. Incoherence is visible: when the choices do not add up, the strategy is not a system — it is a list.

**Strategic moats.** Winning positions must be sustained. Martin and Lafley distinguish between advantages that competitors can copy quickly (and are therefore not lasting advantage) and those that are structurally hard to replicate — because they depend on proprietary data, scale effects, brand, or deeply embedded capability. The strategy must explicitly address how the winning position will be defended over time.

**The strategy of a product within a portfolio.** Within a large company, product strategy must be consistent with the corporate strategy's where-to-play and how-to-win choices — and must reinforce the capabilities the parent company needs. Product teams that optimize for their own metrics without reference to how they serve the company's strategic system often win locally while losing systemically.

<SectionLabel>The one thing</SectionLabel>

Strategy is not planning — it is making specific, integrated choices about where to compete and how to win, then building everything else to reinforce those choices.
