<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

The dilemma Christensen identified in disk drives and excavators now plays out in software at compressed timescales. AI is a disruptive technology in the classic Christensen sense: it enters markets at the low end (cheap, good-enough, often worse on incumbent metrics), improves rapidly on its own trajectory, and eventually overshoots incumbents' needs from below. Companies that dismiss AI-native competitors as inadequate are exhibiting the precise response pattern that Christensen documented in every major disruptive wave. The lesson is not that disruption is inevitable — it is that surviving it requires deliberately cannibalizing yourself before a disruptor does it for you.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

Christensen distinguishes between two fundamentally different innovation types. **Sustaining innovations** improve products along the performance dimensions that existing mainstream customers value — faster processors, higher resolution displays, lower error rates. Incumbents are excellent at sustaining innovation; their customers pull them toward it, and their processes and incentives are built for it. **Disruptive innovations** begin by offering inferior performance on mainstream metrics but superior performance on different metrics — cheaper, simpler, more convenient, good enough. They create new markets or capture low-end segments that incumbents are happy to cede. Then they improve. By the time they compete on mainstream dimensions, incumbents have lost the market context needed to respond.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Incumbents lose to disruption not because they are inattentive but because their rational response to rational customer signals leads them away from the disruptive opportunity — every step of the way.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Value Network

Every company exists within a value network — the context within which it identifies customer needs, solves problems, procures inputs, responds to competition, and pursues profit. A company's value network determines which opportunities are attractive and which are not. Disruptive technologies look unattractive from within the incumbent's value network: they serve lower-margin customers, address smaller initial markets, and perform worse on metrics the mainstream values. The disruptive company is operating in a different value network where these properties are fine. The incompatibility of value networks is what makes rational incumbent behavior produce irrational competitive outcomes.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** The same market data looks different from different value networks. An incumbent and a disruptor looking at the same low-end segment will reach opposite conclusions about its attractiveness — and both will be right given their respective cost structures.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Resource Allocation Problem

Christensen's most important contribution to organizational theory is the resource allocation process as the source of the dilemma. Disruptive opportunities are small initially, low-margin, and uncertain. Sustaining opportunities are large, higher-margin, and supported by existing customers. Companies allocate resources — capital, engineering, attention — through processes that rationally favor sustaining investments over disruptive ones at every decision point. No individual is making a bad choice; the system is making a bad choice. The implication is that organizational structure, not individual awareness, determines the outcome.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Disruption is not an awareness problem — it is a resource allocation problem. Autonomous organizational units with their own P&L and incentive structures are more likely to pursue disruptive opportunities than centralized teams optimizing for the core business.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Responding to Disruption

Christensen offers two responses that have different prerequisites. **Spinning out an autonomous organization** with its own cost structure, customer relationships, and success metrics allows a disruptive business to develop on its own trajectory without being killed by the parent's resource allocation process. This works when the disruptive market is genuinely separate. **Acquiring the disruptor** works when the incumbent can provide resources the disruptor lacks — distribution, brand, capital — without corrupting the disruptive business model. The response that fails is integrating the disruptive effort into the existing organization and expecting it to win against the resource allocation process.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** The only way to fight the resource allocation problem is to structurally separate the disruptive business from the processes that would starve it.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**Overshoot.** Sustaining innovation eventually overshoots what mainstream customers need. Once a product is "good enough," continued improvement on incumbent metrics no longer creates value for most customers — and the basis of competition shifts to price, convenience, or reliability. The moment of overshoot is when disruption from below becomes viable: the market is no longer rewarding the improvements incumbents are best at making.

**The customers you don't have.** Disruptive opportunities often lie with non-consumers — people who would benefit from a product that does not exist yet or who are currently served by an imperfect substitute. Incumbents systematically ignore these customers because their surveys, focus groups, and sales data only capture existing customers. The disruptive company starts with non-consumers and works up.

**Performance trajectory mismatch.** Disruptive technologies improve faster than incumbents expect and faster than the mainstream market requires. By the time a disruptive technology reaches mainstream performance levels, the incumbent has had years of rational signals that it was not a threat. The improvement trajectory, not current performance, is the relevant metric for assessing disruptive risk.

<SectionLabel>The one thing</SectionLabel>

The companies most at risk from disruption are the ones doing everything their customers are asking for — because their customers cannot see the threat coming either.
