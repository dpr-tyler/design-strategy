<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

Cialdini's principles were derived from observing salespeople, marketers, and compliance professionals in the analog world. Forty years later, every digital product has become a more precise delivery mechanism for the same triggers — at scale, with personalization, and with A/B testing to optimize click rates. Understanding influence is now prerequisite to responsible product design: the principles that build trust when used in alignment with users' interests become dark patterns when deployed against them. The question is not whether your product uses these mechanisms — it does — but whether it uses them ethically.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Six Principles of Influence

**Reciprocity** is the obligation to return what we have received. Free samples, free trials, and free content create a felt obligation that increases compliance with subsequent requests. The gift must feel genuine — calculated reciprocity is recognized and resented. **Commitment and consistency** operate on the principle that people align future behavior with prior commitments. Small initial agreements (foot in the door) escalate to larger ones; public commitments are more binding than private ones. **Social proof** tells people what is normal — when uncertain, they look to what others are doing. Review counts, testimonials, and "people like you also bought" all activate this. **Authority** confers trust on experts, credentials, and institutions. Well-sourced product claims, expert endorsements, and visible expertise signal that compliance is reasonable. **Liking** — people comply more readily with requests from people they like. Familiarity, similarity, and physical attractiveness all increase liking. **Scarcity** makes items more desirable when they are perceived as rare or dwindling — "only 3 left" activates it regardless of absolute supply.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** These six principles are not rhetorical tactics — they are automatic psychological responses. They activate compliance before conscious evaluation, which is exactly why they are powerful and ethically demanding.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Fixed-Action Patterns and Triggers

Cialdini draws on ethology to explain the automaticity of influence: animals have "fixed-action patterns" — stereotyped behavioral sequences triggered by specific stimuli (click) that run to completion (whirr). Humans have analogous patterns. The word "because" increases compliance with a request even when the stated reason is trivial. "Expensive = good" is a heuristic that fires automatically in quality-uncertain domains. These triggers exist because they work well most of the time — and that statistical reliability is what makes them exploitable in edge cases.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Influence works because shortcuts are adaptive. The problem is not the shortcut — it is the exploitation of shortcuts by actors whose interests diverge from the user's.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Contrast Principle

Perception is relative. Something presented after an extreme reference point seems more moderate than it would in isolation. A $500 wine option on a restaurant menu makes a $120 bottle seem reasonable. A premium subscription tier makes the mid-tier feel like a bargain. The contrast principle is distinct from anchoring: anchoring shifts evaluation through an initial reference point; contrast shapes perception through adjacent comparison. In pricing, features, or terms, what surrounds an option is as important as the option itself.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Options are not evaluated in isolation — they are evaluated in context. Control the context and you shape the evaluation.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**Weapons of influence vs. honest persuasion.** Cialdini distinguishes between using these principles to help people make decisions that serve their interests (honest persuasion) and using them to extract compliance against users' interests (exploitation). The distinction is not always sharp, but the test is: if the user fully understood the mechanism being used, would they endorse it? Countdown timers on real limited-time offers are honest; fake countdown timers are exploitative.

**Pre-suasion.** Cialdini's later work established that the moment immediately before a message is as persuasive as the message itself. What a user is focused on just before a request shapes how they respond. A charitable organization that asks users to think about a time they helped someone first gets more donations. The implication: the journey to a call to action matters as much as the call to action.

**Social proof calibration.** Social proof is most powerful when the evidence comes from similar others in uncertain situations. "Most popular" works. "Most popular among teams like yours in SaaS" works more. Specificity of the social proof cohort increases its relevance and persuasive force.
