<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

In an era of AI disruption, everyone is doing strategy — and most of it is bad strategy. Rumelt's framework exposes the gap between what organizations call strategy (goal-setting, vision statements, stretch targets) and what strategy actually is (a coherent response to a specific diagnosed challenge). Product leaders under pressure to "define strategy" in quarterly planning cycles are at particular risk of producing what Rumelt calls "fluff" — combinations of buzzwords and ambition dressed up as direction. The kernel framework provides a discipline that is as useful for a product team's quarterly focus as it is for a decade-long competitive positioning exercise.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Kernel of Strategy

Rumelt's kernel has three elements that must be coherent with each other. The **diagnosis** defines or explains the nature of the challenge — not a description of a desired state, but an analysis of what is actually happening that is hard about the situation. A good diagnosis simplifies the complexity of reality by identifying certain aspects of the situation as critical. The **guiding policy** is an approach chosen to cope with or overcome the obstacles identified in the diagnosis — it channels action in certain directions without defining exactly what shall be done. The **coherent actions** are the coordinated set of steps, resource commitments, and practical moves that implement the guiding policy. The test of a strategy is whether these three elements reinforce each other and point in the same direction.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Strategy without diagnosis is just aspiration. Diagnosis without guiding policy is just analysis. Policy without coherent actions is just intent. All three are required — and they must cohere.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Bad Strategy

Rumelt catalogs the hallmarks of bad strategy with bracing specificity. **Fluff** is strategy that uses impressive-sounding words to hide the absence of thought: "Our strategic intent is to be the customers' most preferred provider of seamlessly integrated solutions." **Failure to face the challenge** means that the strategy does not engage the actual difficulties: it announces goals for a better future without addressing the obstacles. **Mistaking goals for strategy** is the most common failure: "Grow revenue 30%" is not a strategy; it is a target. **Bad strategic objectives** are lists of things to do with no unifying logic — a miscellaneous collection masquerading as a plan.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** If your strategy cannot be falsified — if it would be equally true for any company in any situation — it is not a strategy. It is a mission statement.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Leverage and Sources of Competitive Advantage

Good strategy works by exploiting a pivot point — a situation where a small amount of force produces large effects. Rumelt identifies several sources of strategic leverage: **anticipation** (positioning ahead of predictable change), **exploiting pivotal resources** (the unique thing your organization has that others do not), **creating higher demand** (growing the pool rather than fighting for share), **using coherent actions** (reinforcing moves where the whole is greater than the sum of parts). The diagnosis should identify where leverage is available — and the guiding policy should direct resources toward that point.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Strategy is not about doing more things — it is about doing fewer things where you have disproportionate leverage. The coherence of focused action beats the coverage of diffuse effort.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Role of Advantage

Rumelt is skeptical of generic frameworks for competitive advantage and insists that advantage is always specific and contextual. It arises from resources and capabilities that are hard to replicate, combined with demand conditions that make those resources valuable. Understanding your specific advantage — and the specific conditions under which it applies — is more useful than adopting a framework designed for a different industry in a different era. The question is not "what is our competitive advantage?" but "what is hard about our challenge, and what do we have that bears on that hardness?"

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Advantage is contextual and specific. Generic advantage frameworks describe categories — your diagnosis must identify the specific mechanism of advantage in your specific situation.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**The proximate objective.** Good strategy defines objectives that are achievable — close enough to be reachable with current resources, far enough to make a difference. Setting a proximate objective means being honest about what you can accomplish, which requires accurate diagnosis of your situation. Organizations that set wildly ambitious goals without diagnosing the gap between aspiration and capability produce no useful strategy at all.

**Using strength against weakness.** The core logic of strategy is not to meet the enemy on equal terms — it is to arrange situations where your strengths confront their weaknesses. This sounds obvious, but it requires honest assessment of both. Most organizations overestimate their capabilities and underestimate the difficulty of the challenge, which produces strategies built on the wrong asymmetries.

**Entropy and attention.** Organizations naturally drift toward complexity, politics, and incoherence. Strategy is the periodic act of cutting through that drift — saying clearly what matters and what does not, which forces resources toward the critical and away from the comfortable. The discipline of diagnosis is partly about creating shared attention: making explicit what the organization is actually confronting.
