<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

AI has flooded every market category with credible-looking products, collapsing the attention available for any single one. When a prospect can generate a shortlist of ten alternatives in thirty seconds, the question is no longer whether your product works — it is whether the customer can immediately understand what context to place it in and why it belongs there. Dunford's positioning work has become a first-principles survival skill: in a world where distribution is cheap and discovery is algorithm-mediated, products that occupy a vague or contested category position simply disappear. Clarity of context is now a conversion mechanism, not a marketing luxury.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The 5 Components of Positioning

Dunford's positioning framework disassembles the concept into five interdependent components that must all cohere. Competitive alternatives define the baseline: not the products on your competitor slide, but what a customer would actually do if your product ceased to exist tomorrow — which is often a spreadsheet, an email thread, or nothing at all. Unique attributes are the features, capabilities, or characteristics your product has that those alternatives genuinely lack. Value translates those attributes into outcomes the customer cares about — the attribute is "real-time sync across devices"; the value is "you never lose work or context when switching between laptop and phone." The target market characteristic identifies the specific population for whom that value is urgent enough to pay for, because no attribute creates equal value for everyone. Market category is the lens through which all four components are perceived — it sets the customer's expectations, comparison set, and willingness to pay before they read a single word of your copy.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Positioning is not a sentence — it is a set of five interlocking choices that must all point at the same customer in the same moment. Change one component and the others may no longer cohere.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Finding Your Competitive Alternatives

The most dangerous competitor in most markets is the status quo. Dunford insists that teams identify competitive alternatives not by asking "who else sells what we sell?" but by asking "what would the customer do if we didn't exist?" The answer is almost always more disheartening and more useful than the named-competitor answer: customers would use a manual process, build an internal tool, ignore the problem, or use a general-purpose tool in a creative workaround. This matters because positioning against the wrong alternative creates messaging that is irrelevant to the buying decision actually happening. A company that positions against Salesforce when its real alternative is a shared Google Sheet will write copy that impresses no prospect and converts none.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Your real competitive alternative is what the customer was doing before you existed — not who you named on your competitor slide at the last board meeting.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Unique Attributes vs. Value

Attributes are features. Value is why features matter. This distinction is where most product marketing collapses into incomprehensible jargon: teams list capabilities — "enterprise-grade security," "real-time collaboration," "AI-powered recommendations" — and assume the customer will do the translation work themselves. They will not. Dunford's framework requires teams to complete the translation explicitly: the attribute is the fact; the value is the outcome that fact creates for a specific person in a specific situation. "SOC 2 Type II certified" is an attribute; "you can pass your security review without a six-month procurement delay" is value. Positioning lives at the value level — the attribute is the proof point that the value claim is credible, not the claim itself.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Customers don't buy attributes — they buy the outcomes attributes make possible. Positioning that stops at features is leaving the most important work undone.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Market Category Choice

The market category you choose determines who shows up to evaluate your product, what they compare it to, and what price they expect to pay. Dunford argues this choice is one of the highest-leverage and most underexamined decisions in go-to-market strategy. Placing a product in a crowded, established category borrows the customer's existing mental model — they know what the product is for and what good looks like — but requires differentiation against entrenched alternatives. Creating a new category eliminates direct comparison but demands significant customer education before a sale can happen. The right category is not the one your investors prefer or the one that sounds most impressive — it is the one that makes your specific combination of attributes land as obviously valuable to the specific customer who needs them most.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** The market category is a frame, not a label. Choosing the wrong frame makes the product look weak in comparison to alternatives that don't actually serve the same job.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The 10-Step Positioning Process

Dunford's process begins with a diagnostic: understand your current positioning by auditing what you already say and why. The next steps map competitive alternatives, isolate unique attributes, identify the value those attributes create, and characterize the target customers who find that value most urgent. From there, the team finds the market frame of reference — the category — that makes the value land without explanation. The final steps involve testing the positioning with real customers and sales conversations, then operationalizing it across every customer-facing surface: website, pitch deck, demo script, onboarding copy, sales training. The process is iterative and cross-functional: sales data, customer interviews, and win/loss analysis all feed it. Positioning is not finished when it is written — it is finished when it is working in the field.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Positioning is not a creative exercise — it is a research-and-synthesis process that ends with a testable hypothesis. If it cannot be tested against real customer conversations, it is not done.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**Positioning is a team sport, not a marketing function.** The failure mode Dunford documents repeatedly is positioning developed by marketing in isolation and handed to sales as a messaging guide. Sales ignores it because it does not match what customers actually say. Product ignores it because it was not consulted. The only positioning that works is built collaboratively from sales call recordings, customer success notes, and product knowledge — and that requires organizational processes that most companies have not built.

**Bad positioning makes good products invisible.** The damage of poor positioning is not that customers reject the product — it is that they never correctly perceive what it does. A great product in the wrong category will be evaluated against the wrong alternatives, found wanting on dimensions that are irrelevant to its real value, and quietly passed over. The product team blames the sales team; the sales team blames the product. The actual problem is that no one ever did the positioning work.

**The enemy of positioning is trying to be everything to everyone.** Every positioning choice is an exclusion. Defining your target market means explicitly saying that other markets are not your priority. Choosing a market category means accepting the expectations that come with it. Teams that resist these exclusions because they fear leaving revenue on the table produce positioning that is so broad it communicates nothing — and in doing so, they forfeit exactly the clarity that would have converted their best-fit customers.

**Positioning decays.** Markets shift, competitors emerge, and customer language evolves. Positioning that was accurate eighteen months ago may be actively misleading today. Dunford treats positioning as a living document that should be revisited when win rates drop, when sales cycles lengthen, or when a new competitive entrant changes the landscape. The discipline is not writing positioning once — it is building the organizational habits to keep it current.
