<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

The technology adoption lifecycle was mapped decades before AI, but the chasm is still there — and AI-native products are approaching it now. Products that find enthusiastic early adopters among technical users and AI-forward teams often stall when they try to sell to pragmatist buyers who don't care about technology for its own sake and demand complete, proven solutions. Moore's framework explains why growth that feels inevitable to early-stage teams suddenly stops — and what to do about it. The discipline of segment selection, whole product thinking, and positioning-for-pragmatists is as applicable to AI tools as it was to workstations and CRMs.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Technology Adoption Lifecycle

Moore adapts Everett Rogers' adoption curve to the specific context of technology products, identifying five distinct psychographic groups. **Innovators** are technology enthusiasts who adopt for the intrinsic pleasure of exploring new capabilities. **Early adopters** are visionaries who adopt to gain a competitive leap — they tolerate incompleteness because they believe in the transformative potential. **Early majority (pragmatists)** want proven solutions, good references, and vendor stability — they buy when the category is established, not pioneering. **Late majority (conservatives)** are skeptical and buy only when it is riskier not to adopt. **Laggards** resist adoption until forced. The problem is that the needs of early adopters and early majority are so different that the strategies for reaching them are incompatible — hence the chasm.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** The early adopter and the pragmatist are not on a spectrum — they are different buyers with different values, different references, and different risk tolerances. A strategy that works for one will actively alienate the other.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Chasm

The chasm exists between early adopters and the early majority because pragmatists will not buy without references from other pragmatists — but early adopters are not credible references for pragmatists, because pragmatists know that visionaries tolerate incompleteness that pragmatists cannot accept. The result is a gap where early-adopter enthusiasm cannot convert to mainstream traction. Products in the chasm have: customer testimonials from atypical buyers, incomplete product ecosystems, a sales motion optimized for visionary decision-makers, and no established category vocabulary for pragmatist buyers to use when advocating internally.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Early adopter success is not evidence of market success — it is evidence of visionary-buyer success. Pragmatists require a different product posture, different references, and a different sales approach.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The D-Day Strategy: Bowling Pin Targeting

Moore's prescription for crossing the chasm is counterintuitive: instead of expanding broadly, contract to a single, specific, well-defined niche where you can be the undisputed leader. The target should be a segment with a compelling reason to buy, reachable through a focused sales motion, large enough to matter but small enough to win completely. Once you own that beachhead segment — with dominant market share, enthusiastic references, and a complete solution — you expand outward, using each conquest as a reference for an adjacent segment. Like bowling pins, knocking over the lead pin topples the others.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** In the chasm, breadth kills. Concentrated force on a specific, winnable niche produces the references and momentum that broad market coverage cannot.
</FrameworkCallout>

## The Whole Product

Pragmatists do not buy a product — they buy a solution to a problem. The "whole product" is everything required to make the core product deliver its promised value: integrations, training, support, complementary products, community, and professional services. Early adopters assemble the whole product themselves and tolerate gaps. Pragmatists will not. Crossing the chasm requires filling the whole product gap for the target niche before expanding — which means building partnerships, documentation, and support infrastructure that seems premature from a pure product perspective.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** A pragmatist buys a whole product, not a product. If the ecosystem required for success does not exist, it is your problem to create — not theirs to tolerate.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**The bowling pin model.** Each niche conquered provides references and distribution for the next. The choice of the first bowling pin — the beachhead segment — determines the trajectory of market expansion. A bad first niche (wrong reference base, poor growth adjacency) constrains expansion; a good first niche (strong references, natural adjacency to larger markets) unlocks it.

**Pragmatist buying behavior.** Pragmatists use references from other pragmatists in the same industry segment, third-party analyst reports, the company's ability to demonstrate stability (installed base, headcount, customer logos), and completeness of solution. They evaluate risk before value. The sales process for pragmatists is longer, more political, and more reference-dependent than for early adopters — and it cannot be shortcut by product enthusiasm.

**Category leadership.** Pragmatists prefer to buy from the market leader in a clearly defined category — it reduces perceived risk. This means the race to cross the chasm is partly a race to define and own a category before competitors do. Category definition through positioning, analyst relations, and early customer success shapes the vocabulary pragmatists use to evaluate options — and positioning yourself as the leader in a well-defined category is self-fulfilling.

<SectionLabel>The one thing</SectionLabel>

Pick one specific niche, serve it completely, and own it — then use that beachhead to cross into the mainstream. Breadth before depth kills technology products in the chasm.
