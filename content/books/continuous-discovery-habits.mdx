<SectionLabel>Why it matters now</SectionLabel>

The temptation in the AI era is to replace customer research with synthetic research — to use language models to simulate customer responses, generate personas, and predict preferences. Torres' framework argues against this substitution, not because AI simulations are useless, but because the goal of weekly interviews is not information collection — it is team alignment, assumption surfacing, and the development of empathy that changes how teams see their product. A summary generated by a model does not change how a PM thinks about their users the way a live conversation with a frustrated customer does. Continuous discovery is a practice, not a data pipeline.

<SectionLabel>Core frameworks</SectionLabel>

## The Opportunity Solution Tree

The opportunity solution tree (OST) is Torres' central artifact — a living visual representation of a team's discovery work. At the top is the desired outcome the team is pursuing (an OKR-level business or product goal). Below that are **opportunities** — unmet customer needs, pain points, or desires surfaced through research. Below each opportunity are **solutions** — the product ideas the team is considering to address them. Below each solution are **experiments** — the assumption tests designed to validate or invalidate the solution before committing to build. The tree forces several disciplines: tracing every solution to a customer opportunity, tracing every experiment to a specific assumption, and maintaining the scope of discovery to a defined outcome.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** The opportunity solution tree is not a planning artifact — it is a discovery artifact. Its value is in surfacing what you don't know, not documenting what you've decided.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Weekly Customer Interviews

The most operationally demanding element of Torres' framework is the commitment to at least one customer interview per week — not per sprint, not per quarter. The weekly cadence is deliberate: it creates a continuous stream of customer contact that normalizes the practice, keeps the team calibrated on customer reality, and generates enough cumulative interviews to identify patterns without long delay cycles. Torres distinguishes this from project-based research (commissioning a study when you have a decision to make) because project-based research is reactive, infrequent, and disconnected from the team's daily product work. Weekly interviews become the team's peripheral nervous system for customer reality.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Frequency is the point. One interview per week, sustained over months, produces qualitatively different insights than four interviews per month for one month every quarter. Continuity builds cumulative pattern recognition.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Assumption Testing Before Building

Torres frames all product ideas as bundles of assumptions — about desirability (do customers want this?), viability (will the business benefit?), feasibility (can we build it?), and usability (can customers figure out how to use it?). Before committing engineering effort, teams should identify the riskiest assumption for each solution and design the smallest possible test to evaluate it. This is not user testing (testing the solution) — it is assumption testing (testing whether the solution is founded on accurate beliefs). The distinction matters: testing the solution reveals problems late; testing assumptions reveals problems early and cheaply.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Every product idea is a hypothesis with multiple embedded assumptions. Identify the riskiest assumption first, design the smallest test, and let the evidence determine whether to proceed — before building anything.
</FrameworkCallout>

## Representing Opportunities, Not Solutions

A common research failure is asking customers about solutions: "Would you use a feature that does X?" Torres insists on asking about opportunities: "Tell me about the last time you tried to do X. What happened?" Solution-framing questions generate confirmation bias (customers will engage with any plausible feature description). Opportunity-framing questions generate evidence of real friction, workarounds, and unmet needs. The distinction shapes not just interview technique but how teams think about their backlog: opportunities are customer problems to be solved, not features to be shipped.

<FrameworkCallout>
**Key principle:** Interview for stories, not opinions. Ask customers to describe what they did, not what they would do. Behavior is evidence; preference is noise.
</FrameworkCallout>

<SectionLabel>Mental models</SectionLabel>

**The product trio in discovery.** Torres argues that discovery is not a PM solo activity — it is a collaborative practice for the product manager, designer, and tech lead. All three should interview customers (not just observe), synthesize insights together, and co-develop opportunities and solutions. When discovery is siloed to a researcher or PM, it creates a translation layer between insight and decision that distorts both.

**Snapshot vs. continuous.** Most teams do discovery in snapshots — intensive research before a major initiative, then nothing. Continuous discovery changes the team's relationship to uncertainty: instead of waiting until they have "enough" information to decide, they have a permanent, evolving model of customer reality that improves with each interview. Decisions improve because the team's understanding is always current.

**Outcome over output.** The desired outcome at the top of the OST — not features shipped or interviews conducted — is the measure of discovery success. This keeps the team oriented toward business impact rather than research activity. Discovery is not valuable because it surfaces insights; it is valuable because better insights lead to better solutions that achieve the desired outcome.

<SectionLabel>The one thing</SectionLabel>

Interview one customer every week, map what you learn to an opportunity solution tree, and test your riskiest assumptions before you build — every sprint, without exception.
